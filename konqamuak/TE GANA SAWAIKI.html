TE GANA SAWAIKI

By Connor O’Donnell and Roo in 2025

The Protopolynesian language was the language of the original Polynesian people, who lived in Sāmoa and Tonga at the start of the common era. As far as our current knowledge takes us, its ancestors can be traced back to Protoaustrotai, which was spoken in China thousands of years before the common era.

There are no documented records of this language or the people who spoke it. Everything in  this document has been reconstructed by comparing the modern Polynesian languages and  what is already known about its ancestors. Fortunately, correspondences are quite regular and  reveal a lot about how this language must have worked, but there are no guarantees that what  is described in this document is completely accurate. Furthermore, this is not a scientific paper. No attempt has been made here to justify the reconstructions. The purpose of this particular  document is to simply outline a cohesive grammar for reference.

A syllable could only consist of 1 vowel, preceded by an optional consonant, /(C)V/. The  combinations */wu/ and */wo/ never occurred. Each syllable was considered to be consistently  1 mora long. Content words were always made up of at least 2 syllables.

Word stress was nonphonemic and was fixed on the second to last syllable. The stress was  applied after the word had been inflected, so the /si/ was stressed in /siko/, but the /ko/ was  stressed in /siko-fi/.

CONTENT WORDS

There were 2 main subclasses of content words: nouns and verbs. Either could freely function  as modifiers. Nouns, such as */taŋata/ (“person”), could function as heads only in noun  phrases, but verbs could function as heads both in noun phrases and verb phrases.

Verbs could be split even further into 3 subclasses. There were transitive active verbs, such  as */fai/ (“do”, “make”). There were intransitive active verbs, such as */kau/ (“swim”). And  there were stative verbs, such as */ʔuli/ (“[be] black”).

It’s usually easy to tell what subclass words belonged to, based on their semantics.

COMMON MODIFIERS

Protopolynesian was head initial, so common modifers were always placed after their heads.

The adverb position could be used to indicate a collective indefinite patient, without adding a  whole separate noun phrase.

*/mo tee kai moa nai/

 2.DU.NOM PRS eat chicken PQ

Are you eating chicken?

PREPOSITIONS

A preposition was required at the start of every noun phrase.

*/ko/

This was an equative preposition. It was used before each phrase in equative clauses.

*/ko te nei ko te fohe tonu/

 AEQ DEF PROX AEQ DEF paddle correct

These are the correct paddles.

It was also used to specify names.

*/te nuku ko sawaiki/

 DEF island AEQ PN

the island of Savaiʻi

*/ʔe/

This was an ergative preposition. It marked subjects of transitive clauses.

*/naʔa kite ʔe te tautai ʔa sa ika/

 PST see ERG DEF fisherman ABS NDEF fish

The fisherman saw a fish.

*/ʔa/

This had 2 functions. It was an absolutive preposition. It marked subjects of intransitive  clauses, and objects of transitive clauses. (It also marked the accusative when a nominative  pronoun was used.)

*/kua ʔoti ʔa te fono/

 PRF complete ABS DEF meeting

The meeting is over.

*/naʔa kite ʔa sa ika ʔe te tautai/

 PST see ABS NDEF fish ERG DEF fisherman

The fisherman saw a fish.

It was also an alienable possessive preposition. It marked possessors after the phrase  containing the possession. It was used for possessions that are a product of the possessor,  such as children or speech, as well as small items that the possessor has control over and can  easily disown.

*/te ipu ʔa te taʔane/

 DEF cup POSS.AL DEF man

the man’s cup

*/ʔo/

This was an inalienable version of */ʔa/. It was used for possessions that are an integral  component of the possessor, such as body parts or thoughts, as well as drinking water,  clothes, and large items such as boats or houses.

*/ʔi/

This was a general locative preposition. It marked locations, times, or conditions. It could also  be used to express temporary possession. When used with personal names and the  interrogative */hai/, it had the allomorph */ʔia/. When used with personal pronouns, it had the  allomorph */ʔiate/.

*/ʔi t-o ʔo-ku fale/

 LOC DEF-POSS.INAL POSS.INAL-1.SG house

at my house

*/ʔee ʔia tama ʔa te ike/

 PRS LOC PN ABS DEF mallet

Tama has the mallet.

*/ʔee ʔiate koe ʔa te ike/

 PRS LOC 2.SG ABS DEF mallet

You have the mallet.

It could also function ablatively, marking a location that was left.

*/oro mai ʔi te fale/

 go this.way ABL DEF house

Come out of the house.

CONJUNCTIONS

*/ka/

This could be translated as “but”. It indicated that the next clause would subvert the listener’s  expectations.

*/naʔa kou masalo ʔee pono ʔa te ia ka ʔee sala/  PST 1.SG.NOM think PRS true ABS DEF ANA but PRS false I thought that was true, but it’s false.

This could be translated as “and”. It linked sequential actions together.

There were 2 types of reduplication, which were both used exclusively on verbs. MONOMORAIC

Monomoraic reduplication was used when the verb had plural subjects, or when it modified a  plural head. The second to last mora of the uninflected verb was reduplicated.

PHRASE ORDER

The most common order that phrases were put into was verb, subject, object. However, the  subject and object could easily switch places, especially if additional phrases were going to  branch off the subject.

Phrases could be topicalised at the start of the clause with the preposition */ko/, leaving their  original preposition in its place with */ai/ as a relative placeholder head.

*/naʔa ra ka<ka>u ʔi te mataafaŋa/

 PST 3.DU.NOM swim<PL> LOC DEF beach

They swam at the beach.

*/ko te mataafaŋa naʔa ra ka<ka>u ʔi ai/

 AEQ DEF beach PST 3.DU.NOM swim<PL> LOC REL

At the beach, they swam.

That last example could also be interpreted as a relative subclause construction, meaning “the  beach at which they swam”. Subclauses were not demarcated by anything other than their own tense aspect mood particle.
